# Sleeper Dynasty Weekly Strategy Guide

This is the best evidence-backed operating system I would use—not because any study proves each action causes championships, but because the available Sleeper data consistently associates success with **continuous, purposeful roster activity**, especially waivers and trading. Champions averaged about **19.75 moves per season: 16.33 waiver claims and 3.42 trades**, roughly 21% more activity than non-winners. Longer-term winners also traded more in the seasons preceding their championships. ([Dynasty Dealmaker][1])

The goal is **not** to manufacture transactions. It is to systematically inspect every source of new information before the rest of your league fully prices it.

## Your weekly scoreboard

Every week, produce five decisions:

1. **Best starting lineup**
2. **Ranked waiver plan**
3. **One or more roster-spot upgrades**
4. **Current competitive lane**
5. **At least one trade-market observation**

A reasonable season benchmark is:

* **15–20 meaningful waiver/roster moves**
* **3–5 completed trades**
* **One deliberate roster-lane decision by approximately Week 6**
* **No permanently dead roster spots**

Those targets are close to observed champion behavior, but treat them as an engagement benchmark—not a quota. ([Dynasty Dealmaker][1])

# Tuesday: The main strategy day

## Tuesday morning: Review what actually changed

Do not begin with fantasy points. Begin with **opportunity and role changes**.

Review:

| Position | Most useful early signals                                                                  |
| -------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| RB       | Snap share, routes, two-minute work, goal-line use, pass protection, injuries ahead of him |
| WR       | Routes per team dropback, target share, first-read targets, air yards, rookie role growth  |
| TE       | Route participation, slot/wide usage, targets per route, red-zone routes                   |
| QB       | Starting security, injury runway, designed rushing, pressure/sack performance              |
| All      | Coach comments, depth-chart movement, transactions and upcoming schedule                   |

The waiver-wire edge generally comes from recognizing a role change **one week before it becomes obvious in fantasy points**.

### Make three lists

**Add now**

Players whose probability of becoming useful materially increased this weekend.

**Watch**

Interesting players who need one more week of usage confirmation.

**Exit**

Your three least valuable rostered players, ranked in drop order.

This prevents the common mistake of identifying a good pickup but refusing to drop an increasingly worthless bench asset.

## Reclassify your team

Every Tuesday, assign yourself one temporary status:

### Contender

At least two of these are true:

* Top four in record
* Top four in points or maximum points
* Healthy starting lineup projects among the league’s best
* You have credible depth through the playoffs

### Rebuilder or retooler

At least two are true:

* Bottom half in maximum points
* Multiple important players will not help this season
* Your lineup depends on several unexpected breakouts
* Your roster’s trade value is considerably stronger than its scoring ability

### Uncertain

Your record and underlying roster strength disagree.

An uncertain team should generally make **liquid, value-positive moves**, not aggressively buy veterans or tear everything down. Reassess for another week or two.

Do not remain uncertain indefinitely. Sleeper data shows a U-shaped relationship between activity and finish: contenders trade to improve, rebuilding teams trade to restructure, while fifth-through-eighth-place teams are comparatively inactive. That does not prove passivity causes mediocrity, but it strongly supports making a deliberate choice rather than drifting. ([Dynasty Dealmaker][2])

## Tuesday afternoon: Build waiver bids

For a $1,000 dynasty FAAB budget, I would use these operational tiers:

|           Bid | Use case                                                                                       |
| ------------: | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
|    **$0–$10** | Deep stashes, speculative backup, practice-squad promotion                                     |
|   **$11–$25** | Legitimate bench upgrade or increasingly interesting role                                      |
|   **$30–$75** | Strong evidence of a sustainable role, but uncertain starting value                            |
| **$100–$250** | Likely starter created by injury, depth-chart change or unexpected breakout                    |
|     **$300+** | Rare young player with plausible long-term starting value who should never have been available |

Approximately 71% of dynasty additions in one 340,000-add dataset cleared for $25 or less on a $1,000 budget. The practical implication is to **churn cheaply most weeks and spend heavily only when the player belongs to a fundamentally different tier**. ([FantasyPros][3])

### Use contingent bids

Do not submit one claim. Build a sequence:

1. Add Player A, drop Roster Spot 1
2. Add Player B, drop Roster Spot 1
3. Add Player C, drop Roster Spot 1
4. Add Player D, drop Roster Spot 2
5. Add Player E for $0, drop Roster Spot 3

Your objective is to leave every waiver run with **some improvement**, not necessarily the week’s most discussed player.

### Prefer these dynasty waiver archetypes

For contenders:

* Running backs one injury from meaningful work
* Backup superflex quarterbacks
* Players with immediate usable volume
* Direct insurance for genuinely irreplaceable starters
* Players with favorable playoff-week roles

For rebuilders:

* Rookie and second-year receivers gaining routes
* Athletic tight ends earning full-time participation
* Injured young players dropped by impatient managers
* Backup quarterbacks with a plausible future starting path
* Players whose market value can rise even without entering your lineup

Avoid using every bench spot on players who need three unrelated events to become relevant.

## Tuesday evening: Open the trade market

Do not send five calculator-generated offers. Take one focused action:

* Ask one manager how they value a specific player.
* Send one tailored two-for-one proposal.
* Put one player on the trade block with an explicit desired return.
* Ask a rebuilding team whether a veteran is available.
* Ask a contender whether they would pay for your currently productive veteran.

The weekly requirement is **one market interaction**, not one completed trade.

Over a season, target approximately four completed trades. Successful managers in the longitudinal Sleeper dataset averaged around 4.38 annual trades across the three-season window preceding a title, although this remains observational and probably also measures manager engagement and skill. ([Dynasty Dealmaker][1])

# Wednesday: Exploit the waiver aftermath

## Immediately after waivers process

Review:

* Every successful claim
* Every surprisingly expensive bid
* Every dropped player
* Which managers failed to address obvious weaknesses
* Which managers now have roster congestion

The **dropped players** are often more interesting than the original waiver targets.

Set reminders for players who must clear waivers again. Search for veterans dropped to make room for the week’s exciting newcomer.

## Make the post-waiver move

There are three common opportunities:

### The free pickup

A watch-list player received no claims and can now be added for nothing.

### The overreaction drop

A useful player was released after one poor game, an injury scare, or a rookie hype cycle.

### The duplicate-position trade

A manager added another running back or quarterback and is now overloaded at the position. Contact them before they decide what to do.

This is also the day to place injured players into IR and refill the resulting roster opening immediately.

# Thursday: Starting lineup and information advantage

## Thursday morning: Produce the first lineup

Set your lineup using:

* Consensus projection
* Expected opportunity
* Injury status
* Game environment
* Your likely matchup needs

Then identify every decision that remains unresolved.

For each close call, write the condition that decides it:

> Start Player A unless the starting running back is active without limitations.

That is better than reconsidering the entire lineup emotionally on Sunday.

## Preserve flexibility

A Thursday player should usually occupy their specific positional slot rather than a flex spot.

For example:

* Thursday WR → WR slot
* Thursday RB → RB slot
* Sunday/Monday player → flex

That preserves more replacement options if something changes later.

Avoid starting a questionable Thursday player merely to “get someone into the lineup.” Thursday usage creates information disadvantages: you are committing before most of the week’s injury reports are complete.

## Thursday evening: Watch for role confirmation

During the game, ignore isolated touchdowns. Note:

* Who starts
* Who plays passing downs
* Who receives two-minute work
* Who remains on the field after mistakes
* Whether a player’s recent usage trend continues

Move interesting players onto your watch list during the game. Do not send reactionary trade offers during a two-touchdown first half.

# Friday: Trade and portfolio day

Friday is when you evaluate the roster as a collection of assets rather than a lineup.

## Contender actions

Buy only when the acquired player does at least one of these:

* Immediately enters your starting lineup
* Provides high-value insurance at a fragile position
* Substantially protects you against an identified playoff risk
* Can remain valuable beyond the current season

Do not spend a second-round pick merely to improve your eighth bench player.

A first-round pick should generally acquire either:

* A meaningful weekly starter with more than a short rental window, or
* A player whose addition materially changes your championship outlook

The Sleeper analysis found champions were net spenders of picks, but that finding is particularly vulnerable to reverse causality: strong teams have more reason to buy. The right lesson is **spend picks when you possess a real championship window**, not “future picks are bad.” ([Dynasty Dealmaker][1])

## Rebuilder actions

Sell:

* Aging running backs with current volume
* Veteran receivers having touchdown-driven seasons
* Fragile short-term quarterback production
* Replaceable tight ends experiencing temporary target spikes
* Handcuffs that are suddenly starting

Buy:

* Injured young players
* Depressed rookies with intact underlying profiles
* Future picks
* Players whose value timeline matches yours
* Multiple liquid assets in exchange for one declining producer

A rebuilder’s weekly goal is not to lower this week’s score. It is to convert **expiring production into durable market value**.

# Saturday: Build an asymmetric bench

Saturday is the ideal day to replace low-upside depth with players whose value could change on Sunday.

Prioritize:

1. Backup running backs behind injured or questionable starters
2. Superflex backup quarterbacks behind vulnerable starters
3. Young receivers whose route participation is climbing
4. Players returning from IR
5. Athletic tight ends moving toward full-time usage

The guiding question is:

> What could happen Sunday that would make this player substantially more valuable by Tuesday?

“Could score six points if needed” is not enough for the bottom of a dynasty bench.

A third-string veteran receiver with a stable low-value role generally has less bench utility than a backup running back who becomes a starter if one event occurs.

# Sunday: Execution and rolling optionality

## Ninety minutes before the early games

Complete the inactive sweep:

* Confirm every starter is active.
* Verify reported limitations.
* Check offensive-line absences.
* Confirm weather only where genuinely extreme.
* Move late-game players into flex positions.
* Replace doubtful players before their alternatives lock.

## Between game windows

Where your league’s settings permit it, use unlocked bench spots to rotate failed lottery tickets into players from the later games.

Example:

* Your early-window backup RB’s starter remained healthy.
* Drop that backup.
* Add a late-window backup RB whose starter has not played.
* Repeat before Monday night when appropriate.

This is not glamorous, but it creates multiple small opportunities from one roster spot.

## During games

Record observations; do not make conclusions from highlights.

A player scoring twice on three touches is less actionable than a player earning:

* 80% of routes
* Goal-line work
* Two-minute snaps
* Eight targets
* A clear promotion over a teammate

You are trying to predict **next week’s waiver market**, not explain this week’s fantasy points.

---

# Monday: Final stash and anti-tilt day

## Before Monday Night Football

Use any expendable spot on a player whose value could change during the game:

* Backup RB
* Backup quarterback in superflex
* Young receiver receiving increased playing time
* Emerging tight end
* Replacement for a player already known to be injured

Once again, prefer a player with a wide range of possible future value over a veteran with a predictable low ceiling.

## After the game

Do not make emotional drops or trade offers.

Update the watch list, note injuries, and defer decisions until Tuesday’s complete review. A player’s poor fantasy score can coexist with excellent underlying usage.

# Seasonal modifiers

## Weeks 1–4: Discovery phase

This is the most valuable period for aggressive **cheap** churn.

Prioritize:

* Unexpected starters
* Rookie participation growth
* Backfield role changes
* Backup quarterbacks elevated to QB2
* Players who were drafted before preseason news changed their perceived value

Do not overreact to touchdowns or one anomalous target total. Week 1 dynasty waiver data showed substantial activity but continued to cluster around inexpensive bids; many early additions were subsequently dropped. ([FantasyPros][3])

## Weeks 5–8: Choose your lane

By approximately Week 6, compare:

* Record
* Total points
* Maximum points
* Starter health
* Positional depth
* Remaining schedule
* Market value of aging players

Then declare:

* **Buy**
* **Sell**
* **Remain liquid for two more weeks**

The bad outcome is not choosing incorrectly. It is repeatedly making small win-now moves while remaining unlikely to win, or selling useful production while possessing a legitimate title roster.

## Weeks 9–13: Conversion phase

Contenders should convert excess future value into points that enter the weekly lineup.

Rebuilders should sell temporary production before the trade deadline removes competing buyers.

Do not wait for the deadline merely because it feels strategic. Winners and non-winners showed very similar concentrations of deadline-period trading in the large Sleeper analysis. The broader distinction was continuing to engage throughout every trading window. ([Dynasty Dealmaker][4])

## Playoffs: Shorten the horizon

Once eliminated teams can no longer improve your competitive position, your useful planning horizon changes.

Contenders should emphasize:

* Direct backups
* Weekly lineup alternatives
* Opponent-blocking acquisitions where justified
* Players with clear playoff roles
* Depth at positions where a late inactive would be catastrophic

Do not cut genuinely valuable dynasty assets merely to acquire a marginal one-week projection advantage.

# The compact weekly checklist

| Day           | Required action                                                                                     |
| ------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Tuesday**   | Review usage, classify team, rank drops, submit contingent waivers, initiate one trade conversation |
| **Wednesday** | Review claims and dropped players, make free-agent additions, fill IR openings                      |
| **Thursday**  | Set preliminary lineup, preserve flex positions, record remaining injury-dependent decisions        |
| **Friday**    | Evaluate one contender/rebuilder trade opportunity                                                  |
| **Saturday**  | Replace dead depth with asymmetric Sunday stashes                                                   |
| **Sunday**    | Inactive sweep, optimize late swaps, monitor roles rather than touchdowns                           |
| **Monday**    | Make final lottery-ticket stash and defer emotional decisions until Tuesday                         |

## The central rule

**Every roster spot must serve one of four purposes:**

1. Start now
2. Insure an important starter
3. Appreciate in dynasty value
4. Become dramatically more useful after one plausible event

Anything serving none of those purposes is the first player you should consider replacing.

This maps cleanly onto the Sleeper system you previously described: a comprehensive **Tuesday waiver-and-trade report**, followed by a narrower **Thursday lineup-and-injury report**.
